A grand tourer is judged by the distance it can put behind it without ceremony. The GT carries a 110 kilowatt-hour battery and returns 690 kilometres on the WLTP cycle — a real, conservative figure, the kind you can plan a night drive around without watching the percentage tick down. That range is the dividend of everything upstream: the 0.19 drag coefficient that stops the car fighting the air, the high-voltage architecture that wastes less of what the pack holds, and the regenerative system that hands energy back every time you slow.

But range is only half the contract. The other half is how quickly you get it back, and this is where the GT's 900-volt architecture earns its keep. On a 350-kilowatt DC fast charger, the pack goes from 10 to 80 percent in eighteen minutes. That is the length of a coffee and a short walk — not an interruption to the drive so much as a pause inside it. You stop because you wanted to, not because the car forced you to. And the GT holds a high charging rate across most of that window rather than tapering early, so the eighteen-minute figure is what you actually experience at the charger, not a peak you touch for a few seconds.

The reason it can accept that much power without harm is thermal. The same liquid-cooling circuit that keeps the motors composed during a hard run keeps the battery in its ideal temperature band while it charges, and the car preconditions the pack on the way to a charger it knows you're heading for — warming or cooling the cells so they're ready to drink the full current the moment you plug in. Fast charging is not a separate trick bolted onto the car. It is the performance cooling system doing a different job.

At home, the story is calmer and just as deliberate. On AC the GT charges overnight to full, so the car you walk out to in the morning is always topped off and always at the right temperature, having quietly managed itself in the dark. For most owners, the public fast charger becomes the exception — reserved for the long crossings the grand tourer was built to make — rather than a weekly chore.

That is the shape of it. 110 kilowatt-hours. 690 kilometres of genuine range. Eighteen minutes from near-empty to nearly full when you need it, and a full pack waiting every morning when you don't. Enough to leave at midnight, cross a country, and never once think about the thing under the floor that's making it possible. Engineered, like everything else on this car, to stay out of your way.
